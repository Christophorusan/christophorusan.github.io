Persona

An advertising agency I founded for culture, media, technology, and crypto. The work was to turn belief into a repeatable system for trust, demand, and scale.

Agency

Persona was built around a simple problem: many projects had good work, but no coherent public shape. They needed an aesthetic people could recognize, a story people could believe in, and a digital presence that could travel across countries without losing meaning.

I made the agency remote and international because the brands we worked on had to be understood across cultures, languages, and media habits. The goal was not to make campaigns look bigger than they were. It was to make them simple enough to repeat, refined enough to trust, and strong enough for people to support.

That logic shaped work for clients including EFG, CaixaBank, BBC, ARTE TV, Institut Français, TEDx Talks, France 24, the Italian government, Arweave, and major European festivals, generating more than 50,000 ticket sales, 50M online views, and up to 3x ROI without turning the agency into a performance-marketing template.

Arweave

With Arweave and AR.IO, the decision was to avoid explaining technology only through code. Infrastructure is invisible by default, but it still has a life: it changes what can be preserved, financed, verified, and trusted.

The launch campaign framed permanent storage through memory, proof, and permanence so non-technical audiences could understand the opportunity without needing to understand the entire protocol first.

The Sagrada Familia x AR.IO work used cultural preservation as the entry point. It made the technology concrete for people who would not respond to infrastructure language alone: investors, institutions, and audiences outside crypto.

The Paragraph report made the protocol layer legible after the narrative was already visible, connecting AR.IO, gateway networks, and Arweave's Q3 update to the broader permaweb stack.

Broadcast, Radio, TV, and Podcasts

I used television, radio, podcasts, online blogs, and press because different regions and age groups trusted different surfaces. A campaign could not depend on one channel if the audience was distributed across countries, habits, and generations.

The sequence mattered. Small tests showed where an organic response was forming, then the best-performing audiences, visuals, ads, and stories received more budget. Each channel created a different kind of proof, and the campaign became a lifecycle rather than a single launch.

For editorial and audio formats, including BBC World Service's Music Life, the decision was curation before amplification. Copywriting and artist curation had to match aesthetics, audience, and narrative, so artists such as Lucas Santtana could contribute to one coherent story instead of appearing as isolated names.

Festival Advertising

For festivals, the main decision was to treat age, region, and audience lifecycle as the market. A beautiful campaign was not enough; I needed to know how large the market was, how much people were willing to spend, and whether a $10 conversion could create a $50 return.

That meant testing first and scaling second. Once a campaign showed organic response, the budget moved toward the audiences, communities, ads, visuals, and stories that could compound.

I avoided broad targeting and campaigns without a coherent aesthetic. The work had to be international, simple to understand, and capable of feeling both modern and historically grounded across countries and languages.

London Jazz Festival x Southbank Centre

London Jazz Festival trusted me with advertising for Royal Festival Hall at Southbank Centre. I moved the campaign toward measurable online demand because the venue already had authority; the missing piece was knowing which audiences could convert at scale.

The decision was to use the institution's credibility as the trust layer, then let testing decide which creative, audience, and story deserved more budget.

Rendez-vous de l'Erdre

For Rendez-vous de l'Erdre in Nantes, the campaign sold out 5,000 seats by starting locally and then widening the market through online ads, radio, and printed press.

The decision was to keep the regional identity intact while making the conversion system measurable. The campaign had to feel cultural, not generic, while still proving where the demand was coming from.

Snape Maltings

Snape Maltings was an 850-seat venue in a remote location, so the problem was not awareness alone. It was distance, intent, and whether the audience had enough trust to travel.

I used BBC Radio and audience development to create legitimacy, then paired it with targeted online ads for people likely to travel for a high-value cultural night.
